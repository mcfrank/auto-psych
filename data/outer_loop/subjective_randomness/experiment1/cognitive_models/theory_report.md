# Theory Report — Experiment 1

## equally_likely
**Hypothesis:** People judge a sequence as more random the closer its proportion of heads is to 50%.
**Motivation:** This is a basic test of representativeness. A perfectly random process generates heads 50% of the time, so people might heuristically judge randomness by checking how close the overall proportion is to 0.5.
**Mechanism:** It computes the absolute difference between the proportion of heads and 0.5 for each sequence, and uses a softmax choice rule to prefer the sequence with the smaller difference.

## alternation_rate
**Hypothesis:** People judge a sequence as more random if its proportion of alternations is closer to their subjective ideal alternation rate.
**Motivation:** It's well-documented that humans expect random sequences to alternate more frequently than they actually do (the gambler's fallacy / alternation bias). This model tests if subjective randomness is driven by an expectation for a specific, typically high, rate of alternations.
**Mechanism:** It computes the absolute distance between each sequence's alternation proportion and a learned subjective ideal rate, choosing the sequence that is closer to the ideal rate.

## bayesian_fair_coin
**Hypothesis:** Observers compare two binary sequences via the log Bayes factor between a fair-coin null and a biased-coin alternative.
**Motivation:** This represents a rational statistical inference approach to subjective randomness (e.g., Griffiths & Tenenbaum, 2001). People might formalize "random" as "generated by a fair coin" and compute the relative probability of the data under a fair coin vs. an unknown biased coin.
**Mechanism:** Computes the log Bayes factor for both sequences comparing a fair coin model (θ = 0.5) to an alternative model with a free bias parameter θ, preferring the sequence that provides stronger evidence for the fair coin model.
