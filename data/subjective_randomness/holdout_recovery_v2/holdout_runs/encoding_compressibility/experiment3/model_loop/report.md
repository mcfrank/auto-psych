## Hypotheses

- **head_balance**: People judge a sequence as more random when its head proportion is closer to 0.5,
assessing perceived randomness solely by whether the sequence looks like it came
from a fair (unbiased) coin and ignoring alternation patterns and run structure.
- **prototype_similarity**: Random-looking sequences are close to a prototype with balanced H/T counts
and an ideal alternation rate.
- **iter0_candidate2**: People judge a sequence as more random when its head proportion is closer to 0.5, because a balanced sequence looks most consistent with a fair coin. On some fraction of trials, however, they lapse — failing to attend to the stimuli — and respond randomly. The core mechanism is pure head-proportion balance (as in head_balance), extended by a single lapse-rate parameter that absorbs inattentive or guessing trials.
- **inner_loop_model**: Best PyMC model found by the inner model-improvement loop.
- **bayesian_diagnosticity**: Random-looking sequences are diagnostic of a fair coin over salient
non-random alternatives: alternating, biased, and streaky generators.
- **iter1_candidate0**: People judge a sequence as more random when its head proportion is closer to 0.5, but their sensitivity to imbalance is quadratic rather than linear: each additional unit of deviation from 50% heads incurs a disproportionately larger randomness penalty, so mild imbalance is nearly ignored while extreme imbalance is strongly penalized. This is a pure balance-checking mechanism whose discrimination power accelerates with distance from the prototype, differing from the linear head_balance model in how imbalance is psychologically scaled.
- **iter1_candidate2**: People judge a sequence as more random when its Shannon entropy is higher — that is, when its head proportion is closest to 0.5 in information-theoretic terms. Unlike a linear balance model, entropy penalizes extreme imbalance (p near 0 or 1) disproportionately more than mild imbalance (p near 0.4 or 0.6), so only strongly biased sequences lose randomness credit while mildly unequal sequences are treated as nearly equivalent to balanced ones. This is a single-cue, head-proportion-only model that refines the linear balance hypothesis with a specific nonlinear (entropy) scaling.
- **max_run_length**: People judge a sequence as more random when its longest consecutive run is
shorter, because a long run is the most compact single-symbol run-length
encoding unit and is the strongest single cue that the sequence is structured.
- **rle_description_length**: People judge a sequence as more random when its run-length encoding requires
more blocks (alternations + 1 normalized by length), predicting a monotonic
relationship between alternation rate and perceived randomness with no ideal rate.
- **iter0_candidate0**: People judge a sequence as more random when its head count is most combinatorially plausible under a fair coin — that is, when the number of heads is closest to the mode of the binomial distribution. They compare two sequences by log C(n, h), the log of the number of equally-likely arrangements with that head count, penalizing imbalance in proportion to its combinatorial implausibility rather than its raw magnitude.
- **iter0_candidate1**: People judge a sequence as less random when it contains detectable periodic structure — a regular repeating pattern is the strongest cue that the sequence was generated by a rule rather than chance. When comparing two sequences, people prefer the one with lower periodicity as the more random-looking option.
- **iter1_candidate1**: People judge a sequence as more random when its alternation rate is closer to a subjective ideal — an internal standard for how often a "typical" random sequence changes between heads and tails. When comparing two sequences, they prefer the one whose alternation rate deviates less from this ideal, treating both too much streakiness and too much regularity as evidence of non-randomness. The ideal rate is a free parameter, allowing the model to capture whether people's internal standard sits at, above, or below the true fair-coin rate of 0.5.
