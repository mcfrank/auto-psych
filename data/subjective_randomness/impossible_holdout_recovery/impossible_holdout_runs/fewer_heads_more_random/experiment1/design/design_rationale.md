# Experiment 1 Design Rationale

## Overview
We designed 30 stimuli pairs to present to participants to evaluate their subjective perception of randomness. The goal is to discriminate between three cognitive models: `prototype_similarity`, `encoding_compressibility`, and `bayesian_diagnosticity`. 

## Stimuli Selection
- **Number of stimuli selected**: 30 pairs.
- **Candidate pool size**: 250 diverse pairs, generated by stratified random sampling of different lengths (4 to 8), H/T balances, and alternation rates.
- **EIG Range**: 0.1642 – 0.2922

## How the Design Discriminates Between Models
The selected stimuli maximize Expected Information Gain (EIG) with respect to the three competing models. The models emphasize different sequence properties for randomness:
1. **Prototype Similarity** focuses on balanced H/T counts and an "ideal" alternation rate.
2. **Encoding Compressibility** penalizes sequences that can be simply described, such as those with long runs, periodicity, or extreme imbalance.
3. **Bayesian Diagnosticity** evaluates how diagnostic a sequence is for a fair coin compared to specific alternatives (e.g., streaky, alternating, or biased generators).

The highest EIG pairs specifically pit highly structured alternating sequences (e.g., `HTHTHTHT`) against highly imbalanced sequences with single runs (e.g., `TTTT`, `HHHHHHH`). The models disagree on the relative subjective randomness of these two extremes. For instance, an alternating sequence might be heavily penalized by `encoding_compressibility` (due to periodicity) and `bayesian_diagnosticity` (due to the alternating generator hypothesis), while a model emphasizing a specific bias or streakiness might rank them differently relative to a pure uniform run like `TTTT`. By asking participants to make forced choices on these contrasting pairs, we can definitively test which features most strongly drive human perception of non-randomness.
