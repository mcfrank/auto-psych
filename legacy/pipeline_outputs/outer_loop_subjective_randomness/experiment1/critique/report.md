# Critique Report — Subjective Randomness Experiment 1

## Summary

Participants (N=5) completed 20 forced-choice trials, each presenting two coin-flip sequences and asking which looks more random. All trials were of the "alternation" stimulus type. Stimuli are unique (sequence_a, sequence_b) pairs, each seen by all 5 participants (100 total responses). Three cognitive models were evaluated: `balance_heuristic`, `alternation_bias`, and `griffiths_representativeness`.

## Model fit

| Model | Log-likelihood | Posterior |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| balance_heuristic | -80.17 | 0.999999 |
| alternation_bias | -100.75 | 0.000001 |
| griffiths_representativeness | -134.79 | 0.000000 |

The log-likelihood difference between `balance_heuristic` and `alternation_bias` is 20.6 — decisive. `griffiths_representativeness` trails by 54.6 log-units and is effectively ruled out. Only `balance_heuristic` carries meaningful posterior mass.

## Model critiques

### balance_heuristic

Four test statistics were proposed before observing p-values. Bonferroni correction: threshold = 0.05 / 4 = **0.0125**.

#### Significant failures

**Test: mean_abs_error** — Mean absolute error between the balance_heuristic's predicted p(left) and the observed proportion choosing left, averaged across all 20 stimuli.

- Observed T: 0.261, Mean under model: 0.173, p = 0.004
- Interpretation: The model's predictions are systematically less accurate than one would expect if the data were truly generated by the balance heuristic. The model produces an average calibration error of 0.17 on its own data, but the actual data shows 0.26 — roughly 50% larger. This confirms that balance alone does not fully account for the choices, and the model leaves a substantial residual unexplained.

**Test: mean_chose_alternating** — For each stimulus pair, the more-alternating sequence is identified by its consecutive-pair alternation rate. The statistic is the mean rate of choosing that sequence.

- Observed T: 0.670, Mean under model: 0.456, p = 0.002
- Interpretation: Participants chose the more-alternating sequence 67% of the time on trials where the two sequences had different alternation rates. Under the balance_heuristic, the expected rate is only 46% (near chance) because the model is blind to alternation structure — it assigns identical scores to, e.g., HTHTHTHT and HHTTHHTT since both have perfect H/T balance. Participants, however, strongly favour the sequence where every successive outcome alternates. This is the model's core failure: it conflates balanced-chunked and alternating sequences.

#### Non-significant results

- **sd_chose_left**: p = 0.709 — The spread of choices across stimuli was consistent with what the balance_heuristic predicts. The model captures the variance in responses adequately.
- **mean_chose_left**: p = 0.040 — There is a weak rightward bias in the data (mean chose-left = 0.55) compared to the model's null mean of 0.46, but this does not survive Bonferroni correction (threshold = 0.0125).

## Conclusions

`balance_heuristic` is the best-fitting model by a large margin, but it fails two out of four posterior predictive checks at Bonferroni-corrected significance. The failures converge on a single mechanism: **alternation preference**. Participants are not only sensitive to the proportion of Heads and Tails (balance), but also to the local sequential structure — they judge perfectly alternating sequences (HTHTHTHT) as more random than equally balanced but chunked sequences (HHTTHHTT), even though the balance heuristic scores these identically.

This is consistent with decades of research showing that people treat alternation as a hallmark of randomness, independent of global balance. The balance heuristic misses this because its score is a function only of overall H-count, not of run structure or transition frequencies.

## Recommendations for next experiment

1. **Add an alternation-aware model**: Extend the balance heuristic with a transition-rate term, or introduce a dedicated model that scores sequences by their fraction of alternating consecutive pairs. A weighted combination (`w * balance + (1-w) * alt_rate`) would be a natural candidate.

2. **Add a run-length model**: People may also penalise long runs of identical outcomes (e.g., HHHHTTTT). A model based on maximum run length or mean run length would test whether it is alternation preference, run aversion, or both that drives the residual.

3. **Design stimuli to separate alternation from balance**: The current design includes many pairs where alternation and balance are correlated. New stimuli should orthogonally vary balance (equal vs. unequal H/T counts) and local alternation (alternating vs. chunked), yielding trials where these cues conflict. This will allow cleaner estimation of each component's weight.

4. **Increase N per stimulus**: With only 5 participants per stimulus, stimulus-level choice proportions are coarse (steps of 0.2). Doubling to 10 participants would provide finer-grained estimates and increase PPC power.
